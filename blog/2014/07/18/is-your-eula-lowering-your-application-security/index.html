Is Your EULA Lowering Your Application Security?

A couple of days ago I tweeted this:

I'm pretty sure when your EULA prohibits reverse engineering your software, all you prevent is people telling you that they reversed it.

— leE Brotherston (@leEb_public) July 15, 2014

Quite a lot of people agreed (at the time of writing it has 40 retweets and 19 favourites, which is quite a lot for me), so I thought that I would expand and explain this in a little more detail.

The End User Licence Agreement (EULA) is something which is fairly common inclusion in commercial applications, and is typically something dreamed up by the legal team to attempt to enforce whatever it is that they feel needs to be added as part of the licencing agreement. What prompted my particular comment was something I noticed in the EULA of an application I was using, which prohibited the user from being able to:

“copy, distribute, publish, reverse engineer, decompile, disassemble, modify, or translate the Software”

A quick search seems to indicate that this is a fairly common clause in a number of EULAs, particularly in the world of gambling (maybe they all use the same template or legal team).

My assumption is that this clause is included in order to prevent people from creating clone clients, perhaps ones which automate betting and casino style gambling or to launder money via online casinos or perhaps just an attempt to hide flaws in the application stack somewhere.

Some initial thoughts:

  • People who are willing to use an application or service for fraudulent means are probably committing crimes such as fraud in the process. It’s unlikely that breaking the terms of the EULA will weigh hevaily on their minds as a deterrent.
  • If people are not comitting a crime like fraud or laundering, but are using tools to game the system, I’m going to wager (pun intended) that they too are not worried about breaking an EULA.
  • People who are seeking to exploit the system in some way, by reverse engineering the application will, go on take a guess….. probably not care about the EULA

However…

Security researchers, for example, are likely to be dissuaded from taking a closer look at your application by an EULA, but they are the very people that you do not want to be dissuaded. You want these people to find the problems and report them to you responsibly and privately. In a perfect world you would have a bug bounty program like Etsy, Facebook or Microsoft. But even without a formal bug bounty program in place, dissuading researchers could well lower your security posture as they will not be able to go looking for the bugs that you probably want and need discovering. Even if they do decide to look, chances are that you have just cut off their only option to report it to you responsibly without fear of some knee-jerk legal retaliation. Instead, as @esSOBI rightly points out:

@leEb_public @thegrugq Do you want to wind up on pastebin? Cause that's how you wind up on pastebin.

— esSOBi (@essobi) July 15, 2014

So, revisit your EULA, it might be being detremental to your application security.
